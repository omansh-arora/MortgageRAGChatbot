# Understanding Mortgage Types in Canada

## Fixed-Rate Mortgages

### What is a Fixed-Rate Mortgage?

A fixed-rate mortgage locks in your interest rate for a specific term, typically ranging from 6 months to 10 years. Your interest rate and payment amount remain constant throughout the term.

### Advantages:
- **Payment stability** - Know exactly what you'll pay each month
- **Budget certainty** - No surprises from rate fluctuations
- **Peace of mind** - Protection if rates rise
- **Simple planning** - Easy to budget long-term

### Disadvantages:
- **Higher initial rates** - Usually higher than variable rates
- **Penalty for breaking** - Can be expensive if you need to break the mortgage early
- **No benefit from rate drops** - You won't benefit if rates decrease

### Best for:
- First-time home buyers who want predictable payments
- Those who plan to stay in their home for the full term
- Risk-averse borrowers
- When rates are expected to rise

## Variable-Rate Mortgages

### What is a Variable-Rate Mortgage?

A variable-rate mortgage has an interest rate that fluctuates with the lender's prime rate, which is influenced by the Bank of Canada's overnight rate.

### Two Types:

#### 1. Adjustable-Rate Mortgage (ARM)
- Payment amount changes with rate changes
- When rates drop, you pay less; when they rise, you pay more
- More immediate impact on your budget

#### 2. Fixed-Payment Variable Rate
- Payment stays the same, but the interest/principal split changes
- When rates rise, more goes to interest, less to principal
- Could trigger payment adjustment if interest exceeds payment

### Advantages:
- **Lower initial rates** - Typically 0.5% to 1% lower than fixed rates
- **Potential savings** - Save money if rates stay stable or drop
- **Lower penalties** - Usually three months' interest if breaking early
- **Flexibility** - Often easier to convert to fixed rate

### Disadvantages:
- **Payment uncertainty** - Monthly costs can increase
- **Budget risk** - Harder to plan if rates rise significantly
- **Stress** - Can cause anxiety for some borrowers

### Best for:
- Those with flexible budgets and stable income
- Risk-tolerant borrowers
- Those who believe rates will decrease or stay stable
- Borrowers planning to pay off mortgage quickly

## Hybrid/Combination Mortgages

### What is a Hybrid Mortgage?

Split your mortgage between fixed and variable rates to balance risk and potential savings.

### Example Structure:
- 50% fixed at 5.5% 
- 50% variable at 4.5%
- Effective rate: 5.0%

### Advantages:
- **Balanced approach** - Get stability and potential savings
- **Risk mitigation** - Not fully exposed to rate increases
- **Flexibility** - Can adjust split based on market outlook

### Best for:
- Those who want some protection but also some savings potential
- Moderate risk tolerance
- Uncertain market conditions

## Open vs. Closed Mortgages

### Closed Mortgage

**Features:**
- Committed for full term
- Lower interest rates
- Limited or no prepayment options beyond allowances
- Breaking mortgage triggers penalties

**Penalties for breaking:**
- Fixed rate: Greater of three months' interest OR Interest Rate Differential (IRD)
- Variable rate: Typically three months' interest

**Best for:** Most homeowners staying for the full term

### Open Mortgage

**Features:**
- Can pay off any amount anytime without penalty
- Higher interest rates (typically 1-2% more than closed)
- Complete flexibility
- Can switch lenders anytime

**Best for:** 
- Short-term situations
- Expecting major life changes
- Planning to sell soon
- Expecting large lump sum (inheritance, bonus)

## Specialized Mortgage Products

### 1. High-Ratio vs. Conventional Mortgages

**High-Ratio (Less than 20% down):**
- Requires mortgage default insurance (CMHC, Sagen, Canada Guaranty)
- Insurance premium: 2.8% to 4.0% of mortgage amount
- Maximum 30-year amortization
- Maximum purchase price: $1,000,000

**Conventional (20% or more down):**
- No mortgage insurance required
- More flexible amortization options (up to 30 years standard, sometimes longer)
- No purchase price limit
- Slightly better interest rates

### 2. Cashback Mortgages

**Features:**
- Receive cash (1% to 7% of mortgage amount) at closing
- Higher interest rates to compensate
- Must stay with lender for full term or repay cashback

**Best for:**
- Those short on closing costs
- Need immediate funds for renovations
- Have accounted for higher long-term costs

### 3. Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)

**Features:**
- Borrow against home equity (up to 65% of home value)
- Interest-only payments
- Revolving credit - borrow, repay, re-borrow
- Variable interest rate (typically prime + 0.5%)

**Best for:**
- Home renovations
- Debt consolidation
- Investment purposes
- Emergency fund access

### 4. Reverse Mortgages

**Features:**
- For homeowners 55+ years old
- No monthly payments required
- Loan repaid when you sell, move, or pass away
- Can access up to 55% of home value

**Best for:**
- Retirees needing income
- No monthly payment ability
- Plan to stay in home long-term

## Amortization Periods

### What is Amortization?

The total length of time it will take to pay off your mortgage completely.

### Common Amortization Periods:

**25 Years (Most Common):**
- Balance between payment size and total interest
- Default for most mortgages

**30 Years:**
- Lower monthly payments
- More total interest paid
- Available for high-ratio insured mortgages

**20 Years or Less:**
- Higher monthly payments
- Significant interest savings
- Build equity faster

### Example Comparison ($400,000 mortgage at 5.5%):

| Amortization | Monthly Payment | Total Interest Paid |
|--------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| 30 years     | $2,271         | $417,560           |
| 25 years     | $2,453         | $335,900           |
| 20 years     | $2,747         | $259,280           |
| 15 years     | $3,271         | $188,780           |

**Savings (15 vs 30 years): $228,780 in interest!**

- 12 payments per year
- Most common option
- Easiest to budget

## Prepayment Privileges

Most closed mortgages allow prepayments without penalty:

### Common Privileges:
- **Lump sum payments:** 10-20% of original principal per year
- **Payment increases:** Increase regular payments by 10-20% per year
- **Double-up payments:** Make extra payments equal to regular amount

### Benefits of Prepayments:
- Reduce amortization period
- Save thousands in interest
- Build equity faster
- More flexibility

### Strategy Tips:
1. Use tax refunds for lump sum payments
2. Increase payments when you get a raise
3. Apply bonuses directly to mortgage
4. Make prepayments early in mortgage term for maximum impact

## Choosing the Right Mortgage

### Consider These Factors:

1. **Risk tolerance** - How comfortable are you with payment uncertainty?
2. **Financial situation** - Do you have budget flexibility?
3. **Timeline** - How long will you keep this mortgage?
4. **Market outlook** - Where are rates heading?
5. **Life plans** - Any major changes expected?

### Questions to Ask:

- What's my payment with each option?
- How much could my payment increase if rates rise?
- What are the penalties for breaking early?
- What prepayment options are available?
- Can I convert from variable to fixed later?

## Important Reminders

- **Stress test requirement:** Must qualify at higher rate (currently your rate + 2% or 5.25%, whichever is higher)
- **Rate holds:** Most lenders hold rates for 90-120 days
- **Shop around:** Rates and terms vary significantly between lenders
- **Read the fine print:** Understand all terms and conditions
- **Plan for renewal:** Start looking 120 days before term ends
